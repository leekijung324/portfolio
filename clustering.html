Clustering Wines Based on Chemical Components

Ki Jung Lee | October 22, 2025

Summary

This case study explores the application of unsupervised machine learning techniques, specifically hierarchical and K-means clustering, to analyze the chemical composition of wines and identify distinct groups. The analysis utilizes a dataset containing 13 chemical attributes of 178 wines. Exploratory data analysis (EDA) techniques, including histograms, boxplots, scatterplot matrices, and correlation analysis, were employed to gain insights into the data's distribution, relationships, and potential patterns.

The clustering results reveal three distinct wine groups, each with unique chemical profiles. These clusters potentially represent different wine types, suggesting that chemical composition plays a significant role in determining a wine's characteristics. This study highlights the potential of clustering algorithms in wine classification and contributes to the ongoing research in understanding the complex relationship between wine chemistry and its sensory attributes.

1. Introduction

The wine industry faces challenges in understanding and communicating the complex relationships between a wine's chemical composition and its resulting flavor profile, quality, and market value. While winemakers and sommeliers possess expert knowledge, there is a lack of accessible, data-driven tools to classify wines objectively and consistently.

Current methods of wine classification often rely on subjective descriptions, regional designations, or grape varietals, which may not fully capture the nuances of individual wines. This can lead to confusion, mismatched expectations, and missed opportunities for discovering new and exciting flavors.

3. Data Description

The dataset includes the following 13 chemical attributes:

  • Alcohol: The percentage of alcohol content.
  • Malic Acid: A tart-tasting organic acid.
  • Ash: The inorganic residue remaining after incineration.
  • Alcalinity of Ash: A measure of the alkalinity (basicity) of the ash.
  • Magnesium: The amount of magnesium, an essential mineral.
  • Total Phenols: The total amount of phenolic compounds (e.g., tannins, flavonoids).
  • Flavanoids: A specific group of phenolic compounds with antioxidant properties.
  • Nonflavanoid Phenols: Phenolic compounds that are not flavonoids.
  • Proanthocyanins: Another class of phenolic compounds, contributing to astringency.
  • Color Intensity: A measure of the depth of the wine's color.
  • Hue: A measure of the dominant wavelength of the wine's color.
  • OD280/OD315 of Diluted Wines: A ratio related to the protein content.
  • Proline: The amount of proline, an amino acid influencing mouthfeel.

7. K-Means Clustering

The Elbow Method was used to determine the optimal number of clusters (k).

# Elbow Method
wcss = []
for i in range(1, 11):
    kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=i, init='k-means++', random_state=42)
    kmeans.fit(wine_df_scaled)
    wcss.append(kmeans.inertia_)

# Plot the Elbow curve
plt.figure(figsize=(8, 6))
plt.plot(range(1, 11), wcss, marker='o', linestyle='--')
plt.xlabel('Number of Clusters (k)')
plt.ylabel('Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS)')
plt.title('Elbow Method for Optimal k')
plt.show()

The chart shows a distinct "elbow" at k=3, suggesting this is the optimal number of clusters. Silhouette plots also confirmed that a 3-cluster solution is optimal, as all clusters showed above-average silhouette scores with minimal size fluctuation.

K-Means with k=3

# Perform K-Means with 3 clusters
kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=3, random_state=42)
wine_df_scaled['Cluster'] = kmeans.fit_predict(wine_df_scaled)

# Plot mean values for each cluster
plt.figure(figsize=(12, 6))
for cluster_label in wine_df_scaled['Cluster'].unique():
    cluster_data = wine_df_scaled[wine_df_scaled['Cluster'] == cluster_label]
    cluster_mean = cluster_data.drop(columns=['Cluster']).mean()
    plt.plot(cluster_mean.index, cluster_mean.values, marker='o', linestyle='-', label=f'Cluster {cluster_label}')

plt.xlabel('Feature')
plt.ylabel('Mean Value (Standardized)')
plt.title('Mean Values of Features by Cluster')
plt.xticks(rotation=45)
plt.legend()
plt.show()

8. Conclusion & Cluster Profiles

The plot of mean values vividly illustrates the distinct chemical profiles of the three wine clusters, confirming they represent wines with unique characteristics.

  • Cluster 0 (High Alcohol, Proline, Color Intensity): Alcohol, proline, and color intensity stand out. This suggests wines in Cluster 0 are likely fuller-bodied, more intense in color (perhaps indicating red wines), and potentially have a richer, more complex flavor profile.
  • Cluster 1 (High Flavanoids and Phenols): Total phenols, flavanoids, and proanthocyanins are noticeable. These are all phenolic compounds contributing to astringency, bitterness, and complexity. Wines in this cluster might have more pronounced tannins, a firmer structure, and a greater potential for aging.
  • Cluster 2 (Moderate Profile): This cluster has slightly elevated ash and magnesium content. It might represent a "middle ground" with a more balanced profile. The elevated minerals might contribute subtle nuances to the taste and texture.
